# Framework — Value Ladder (Russell Brunson)

## Origin
Russell Brunson, *DotCom Secrets* (2015, expanded ed. 2020). The
core funnel-design model used by ClickFunnels and most info-product
businesses. The premise: customers don't buy your highest-ticket
offer first. They climb a ladder — each rung delivers more value at
a higher price, and each rung qualifies them to ascend.

## When to use
- Designing a multi-tier offer suite (free → low → mid → high)
- An info-product, course, coaching, agency, or SaaS with services
- You have a single offer and conversion is stuck — you may need a
  lower-priced "front-end" to acquire customers
- LTV is too low — you need higher rungs to climb

## When NOT to use
- Pure self-serve SaaS with no human-touch upgrade path (a ladder
  exists but it's the pricing tiers, not a Brunson-style funnel)
- Single-purchase products (one rung is fine)

## The ladder shape

```
                                    ┌─────────────────┐
                                    │  $25k+  Mastermind / Done-for-you
                                    └─────────────────┘
                              ┌─────────────────────────┐
                              │  $2k-10k  Coaching / Cohort
                              └─────────────────────────┘
                       ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
                       │  $200-1k  Course / Mid-tier SaaS
                       └─────────────────────────────────┘
                ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
                │  $7-50  Tripwire / Book / Entry SaaS
                └─────────────────────────────────────────┘
        ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
        │  Free  Lead magnet / Free tier / Free trial
        └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Each rung must:
1. **Stand alone** — deliver complete value at that price
2. **Naturally lead** to the next rung (the next pain it creates is
   solved by the next rung)
3. **Qualify** the customer (price filters seriousness; effort
   filters fit)

## The "bait → frontend → middle → backend" pattern

- **Bait** (free): solves a *fragment* of the problem; gets contact
  info or the first interaction. Lead magnet, free tool, free trial,
  free chapter, calculator.
- **Frontend** ($7-50, "tripwire"): converts the lead into a
  *paying customer*. The price is low enough that "yes" is easy. The
  goal is not profit — it's the relationship change. (A buyer
  converts to a higher offer 10-30x more readily than a non-buyer.)
- **Middle** ($200-1k): the core offer. This is usually where the
  business actually makes money. Course, mid-tier SaaS, group
  program.
- **Backend** ($2k-25k+): the high-ticket offer for serious customers.
  Done-for-you services, coaching, masterminds, enterprise. Backend
  often funds the entire ladder.

## The sequencing rule

> "If you have a backend offer but no frontend, you're trying to
> propose marriage on the first date."

Don't expect cold traffic to buy a $5k coaching package. Get them
on the ladder first. Each rung pays for the next rung's customer
acquisition.

## Step-by-step application

### 1. Map customer's transformation journey
Where are they now (problem state)? Where do they want to be
(dream state)? What are the 3-5 stops along the way?

### 2. Design one rung per stop
Each rung addresses ONE stop in the journey. Don't have a $50 offer
and a $5000 offer — too big a leap. Fill the middle.

### 3. Price each rung 5-10x the previous
Standard ratio. $0 → $50 → $500 → $5k. The jumps are intentional —
you're not pricing on cost, you're pricing on value-delivered at
each transformation stage.

### 4. Build the bridge between rungs
At each rung, the natural next pain becomes obvious — and the next
rung solves it. Examples:
- Free book reveals the *strategy* → reader needs *implementation
  help* → frontend course
- Course teaches the method → graduates need *accountability* →
  group coaching
- Coaching exposes the *advanced edge* → top performers want
  *done-for-you* → mastermind / agency tier

### 5. Drive cold traffic to the bait, not the backend
Paid ads, content, partnerships → free offer. Build the email
sequence / nurture flow that walks them up the ladder.

### 6. Measure ascension, not just conversion
Standard funnels measure conversion to *one* offer. Ladders measure
**ascension rate** — what % of buyers at rung N reach rung N+1?
That's your real LTV multiplier.

## Common ladder traps

- **One rung too high**: skipping the frontend means cold traffic
  never converts. Add a tripwire.
- **Two rungs too close**: $100 and $200 isn't a ladder, it's a
  pricing menu. Spread the rungs.
- **Backend before frontend**: you're profitable per sale but
  starving for volume. Build the frontend.
- **Weak bridge**: each rung should *create the next pain*. If your
  course graduates have no clear reason to buy coaching, the bridge
  is broken.

## Output structure

```
## Value Ladder — {project name}

### Customer transformation journey
**From:** {current state — pain, frustration, current alternative}
**To:** {dream state — vivid outcome}
**Stops along the way:** {3-5 milestones}

### Proposed ladder
| Rung | Price | Offer | Solves stop # | Bridge to next rung |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bait | Free | {lead magnet} | {entry pain} | Reveals strategy → need help with… |
| Frontend | ${X} | {tripwire} | {stop 1} | Implementation pain → need a system |
| Middle | ${Y} | {core offer} | {stop 2-3} | Accountability gap → group |
| Backend | ${Z} | {high-ticket} | {stop 4-5} | Scale ceiling → done-for-you |

### Pricing logic
- Frontend: ${X} — calibrated to be a 'no-brainer' for cold buyer
- Middle: ${Y} — anchors the business; ~5-10x frontend
- Backend: ${Z} — funds the rest; 10x middle

### Ascension targets (12 months post-launch)
- Bait → Frontend: {X}%
- Frontend → Middle: {Y}%
- Middle → Backend: {Z}%

### Traffic strategy (to bait only)
- {channel} — bait offer is the conversion event
- Email nurture sequence walks Bait buyers up the ladder

### Riskiest rung + experiment
**Riskiest:** {rung} (most likely to break the funnel)
**Experiment:** {test} in {30/60} days
```

## Decision-log entry suggestion
- Category: pricing or product
- Reversibility: expensive (re-pricing breaks past-customer trust)
- Revisit: 90 days after launching the full ladder
